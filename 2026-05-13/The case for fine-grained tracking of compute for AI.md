### Main Topic: AI Compute Tracking

**Main Thesis:** Current ways of measuring AI hardware progress are inaccurate because they only track theoretical limits and fail to account for how different types of chips and manufacturing processes actually scale.

**Simple Summary:** We currently track AI performance using simple numbers like FLOP/s (theoretical speed), but these methods are flawed. They don't show how much actual work the hardware can do, they don't work well when different types of chips are used, and they miss important improvements made in design and manufacturing. The authors propose creating a new system that builds separate scaling models for each type of chip. This new system will track the real bottlenecks and improvements across the entire process—from the chip design to how it is built and deployed—to accurately predict how AI compute will grow.

**The Bottom Line:** > To accurately predict future AI capabilities, we must stop using single, general metrics and start tracking specific scaling models for each type of hardware to see what is actually achievable.
